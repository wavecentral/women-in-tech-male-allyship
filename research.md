# Building Male Allyship in Austin Technology

## *An Evidence-Based Strategy for the April 2026 Quarterly Meeting*

Prepared by **Mark Garcia**, Co-Lead (since September 2025), Austin Women in Tech Impact Network
March 2026

---

## Abstract

As co-lead of the Austin Women in Tech Impact Network, I undertook this research to inform the strategic design of our April 2026 quarterly meeting, which will experimentally feature an all-male panel exploring allyship, sponsorship, and the active promotion of women in technology. Our current audience composition is approximately ten women for every one man (10:1); our stated goal is to shift that ratio to 10:5. This paper synthesizes findings from national male allyship programming between 2022 and 2025, analyzes strategies that have successfully increased male attendance at gender-focused technology events, and proposes a slate of Austin-based male panelist candidates with documented involvement in advancing women in technology. The analysis draws on published research from Catalyst, the Integrating Women Leaders Foundation, the Society of Women Engineers, AnitaB.org, and behavioral-science surveys of male attendance patterns at diversity events. I conclude with a recommended agenda, recruitment strategy, and measurement framework tailored to our organizational context.

**Keywords:** male allyship, women in technology, Austin tech ecosystem, gender equity, diversity and inclusion, event design, panel programming

## Section 1. Introduction & Research Questions

The Austin Women in Tech Impact Network convenes quarterly gatherings of a hundred or more technologists, educators, and industry leaders in partnership with local leaders of Western Governors University (WGU), our sponsor. For most of our history, our panels have centered the voices of prominent women advocating for women. This has been, and remains, foundational work. But the demographics of our audience tell a harder story: roughly one in ten attendees is a man. If allyship is to be anything more than an internal conversation among women, we need the other half of the workforce in the room.

Our planning committee — comprising myself, my co-lead, and local leaders from WGU — has committed to an experiment for April 2026. Rather than recruiting the usual panel of female advocates, we will feature an all-male panel of Austin technology leaders who can speak publicly and credibly about what it has meant, in their own careers, to support the advancement of women in our industry. Our aspirational goal is a 10:5 gender ratio in the audience, a fivefold increase in male participation, and — more importantly — a shift in the conversation from awareness to action.

This research brief was written to ground that experiment in evidence rather than intuition. Four questions framed my inquiry:

1. What male allyship programs in the United States have produced measurable increases in male participation, and what can we learn from their design?
2. Which topics, panel formats, and session structures have generated the strongest engagement and post-event behavior change?
3. Which men in the Austin technology ecosystem have documented records of supporting women-focused organizations and initiatives, and are strong candidates for our panel?
4. What specific interventions — invitation strategy, framing, timing, venue — are most likely to triple male attendance at a local event like ours?

## Section 2. Research Approach

I reviewed primary and secondary sources covering male allyship events, conferences, research studies, and local Austin coverage from 2018 through early 2026, prioritizing events held between 2022 and 2025 as most representative of current programming norms. For each event, I attempted to document organizing body, panelists, agenda structure, sponsors, attendance demographics, published outcomes, and attendee feedback. I supplemented published event descriptions with research literature on male participation in gender-focused events, notably the behavioral-science survey by Wallaert, Catalyst's annual research on men and gender inclusion, and the Integrating Women Leaders Foundation's State of Allyship-in-Action research study published through Harvard Business Review.

For the Austin panelist analysis, I searched public records, press coverage, and nonprofit board listings for male executives and contributors associated with Austin-based women-in-tech organizations including DivInc, Girlstart, Latinas in Tech Austin, Latinitas, Austin Women in Technology, and the Austin Technology Council. I note openly that public documentation of male allies in Austin is sparse relative to documentation of female leaders in the same organizations — a data gap I treat as a finding in its own right, and which shapes my recruitment recommendations in Part IV.

> **A note on voice and framing**
>
> Throughout this brief I write in the first person because this is a working document for our planning committee, not a detached academic paper. Where I make recommendations, I am speaking as the co-lead proposing a course of action, not as a neutral observer. Readers from outside our Impact Network are welcome to adapt the framework to their own contexts.

## Section 3 · Part I. National Male Allyship Programming, 2022–2025

The landscape of male allyship programming has matured considerably over the past three years. Where earlier events often stopped at awareness-building — convincing men that a gender gap exists — the most effective contemporary programs have moved toward skill-building, scenario practice, and public accountability. Six programs stood out in my review as particularly instructive for our purposes.

| Statistic | What it measures |
|:---------:|:-----------------|
| **97%** | of men say they want to close gender gaps at work |
| **40%** | of male event attendees cite a personal invitation as the deciding factor |
| **2×**  | men rate themselves as active allies at roughly twice the rate women experience them as such |

### 3.1 The IWL ALL IN Allyship Summit

> **IWL ALL IN Virtual Allyship Summit (2022–2024)**
>
> **Organizer:** Integrating Women Leaders Foundation · **Format:** Day-long virtual summit (6.5 hrs) · **Presenting Sponsor (2024):** Amazon Web Services

**Panel Composition.** The inaugural 2022 summit opened with Stedman Graham's keynote on "Identity Leadership and the Allyship Journey" and featured a 90-minute State of Allyship panel built around Dr. Brad Johnson and Dr. David Smith, former U.S. Naval officers and co-authors of *Good Guys: How Men Can Be Better Allies for Women in the Workplace*. Jeffrey Tobias Halter (CEO, YWomen; author of *WHY WOMEN: The Leadership Imperative*) co-presented the foundational session, with Robert Lescano of Amazon Web Services on the closing panel. The 2024 edition added Dave Lavanty (Vice President, AWS ProServe Americas) and Eric Scott (Principal, ZS).

**Agenda Structure.** The day opened with keynote and foundational training, broke into four concurrent breakout tracks (women as allies for other women, allyship and intersectionality, LGBTQIA+ allyship, and allyship to introverts), returned to plenary for the 90-minute State of Allyship panel, and closed with 30 minutes of structured action planning.

**Evidentiary Backbone.** The summit's credibility rested on IWL's State of Allyship-in-Action research study, a survey of nearly 1,200 respondents (62% women, 36% men, 2% non-binary) across mid-market and large enterprise organizations, subsequently published through Harvard Business Review. The study's most cited finding — that men rate themselves as active allies roughly twice as often as women rate them as such — produced what I would describe as *productive discomfort*, reframing allyship from self-identification to measurable behavior.

**What I Take From This Case**

- **Research-grounded content** gave the summit legitimacy that pure storytelling cannot.
- **The virtual format** lowered barriers for men uncertain about attending a women-focused event in public.
- **Structured action planning** in the final 30 minutes produced artifacts participants could carry back to their teams.

### 3.2 Women in Manufacturing: Men as Allies Conference

> **Men as Allies Conference (February 2025)**
>
> **Organizer:** Women in Manufacturing · **Venue:** Hilton Clearwater Beach Resort & Spa, Florida · **Dates:** February 19–20, 2025 · **Audience:** Male directors and above, invitation-only

**Panel Composition.** Rich Stinson (CEO and President, Southwire Company) delivered opening remarks; Barry Powell (President and Regional CEO, Siemens Electrical Products) delivered the closing keynote. Ashan Javed (President, MTE Corporation) anchored the central conversation with what I consider the most quotable framing of male allyship I encountered in the entire review:

> Meaningful allyship requires action, listening and learning. The future of manufacturing depends on harnessing all available talent, and that starts with male leaders stepping up as committed allies.
>
> — *Ashan Javed, President, MTE Corporation*

**Design Choice Worth Emulating.** The organizers made a deliberate choice to target directors and above exclusively. This has two effects: it creates a room in which senior men can speak candidly without fear of being evaluated by their reports, and it concentrates the intervention at the level where organizational change is most likely to originate. For our context, this suggests reserving a small number of seats for mid-level managers and individual contributors by explicit invitation, rather than opening registration to everyone and hoping for the right mix.

### 3.3 Grace Hopper Celebration Male Allies Panels

> **Grace Hopper Celebration (2014–present)**
>
> **Organizer:** AnitaB.org · **Scale:** 20,000+ attendees at peak · **Male Attendance:** ~500 in peak in-person years (roughly 2.5–6%)

The Grace Hopper Celebration introduced the first-ever Male Allies plenary panel in 2014, bringing C-suite leaders from Google, Facebook, GoDaddy, and Intuit on stage for what turned out to be a controversial but formative session. Marc Benioff of Salesforce delivered a co-keynote with then-U.S. CTO Megan Smith. By 2018, the male allies programming had matured into a moderated 90-minute session featuring Matt Wallaert, Haile Owusu, Wayne Barlow, and Evin Robinson, with a room that reached roughly fifty-fifty gender balance.

What I find instructive about the Grace Hopper arc is the organization's willingness to stay with a difficult format through its early missteps. The 2014 panel received significant criticism; AnitaB.org did not abandon the format but iterated on it. By 2018, attendee feedback centered on language I would hope to hear at our own event: *changed perspective*, *willingness to redirect conversations*, *commitment to change teams and impact industry*.

### 3.4 Louisville Women in Technology Conference (RockIT Women)

> **Annual Women in Technology Conference (2022–2025)**
>
> **Organizer:** RockIT Women · **Venue:** Crown Plaza Hotel, Louisville, Kentucky · **Powered by:** Microsoft · **Notable feature:** Annual Male Ally Award

The Louisville conference has made what I consider the most replicable single design choice in the national landscape: an annual Male Ally Award, publicly conferred, alongside awards for senior women leaders and rising stars. The conference's promotional language — "hundreds of women plus a few good men" — does not pretend to equal representation, but it does communicate unambiguous welcome. Men serve on planning committees, participate in volunteer leadership, and are formally recognized for their contributions.

The mechanism matters. Public recognition reframes male allyship from a private virtue into a visible professional identity. For our Impact Network, I believe this is a pattern worth adopting, even if our instance is smaller: an annual recognition of a male ally in Austin technology, named and presented at our first quarterly meeting of each year.

### 3.5 Society of Women Engineers: HeForSWE

> **HeForSWE Affinity Group (2018–present)**
>
> **Organizer:** Society of Women Engineers · **Membership base:** 50,000+ engineers

SWE's approach differs from event-driven programs in one important respect: HeForSWE is a year-round affinity group rather than a standalone conference. Programming at the annual WE conference — including the 2021 session "Leveraging Male Allies to Champion Gender Inclusion and Equity" featuring Dr. Brad Johnson — builds on ongoing community rather than starting from cold each year. Danny Foody of Project Farma at Purdue, a senior manager and current HeForSWE co-lead, describes his motivation in terms I find unusually honest for this domain:

> It's clear that women are underrepresented in STEM fields, and especially in leadership positions. I want to be a part of that process and encourage more men to be a champion and an ally.
>
> — *Danny Foody, Senior Manager, Project Farma*

SWE also addresses, rather than avoids, the psychological barriers that keep men out of allyship work: anti-femininity norms, fear of disapproval from other men, and lack of confidence about "saying the wrong thing." These are not abstract obstacles. They are the reason many of our male colleagues in Austin tech have not attended our previous meetings, and I think we should name them explicitly in our April programming.

### 3.6 University-Based Programs

Three university programs round out the national picture. North Dakota State University's Advocates and Allies program, launched in 2009 with NSF ADVANCE grant funding, offers tiered training — a two-hour ally session for broad participation, and a more intensive Advocate track for institutional change agents. Nearly 90% of participants reported learning something new. Princeton's Male Allies for Women in STEM seminar draws 100+ graduate students, postdoctoral fellows, faculty, and staff each year, with a follow-on coffee hour for men interested in deeper engagement. Indiana University Bloomington's Advocates and Allies for Equity program, led by Dr. Thomas James of the Department of Psychological and Brain Sciences, offers open sessions on "Engaging Men as Allies."

The pattern across university programs is the layered design: a broad entry point followed by an opt-in pathway to deeper commitment. For our Impact Network, this suggests that the April event should not be our only touch. It should be the entry point to a smaller, sustained community of Austin men committed to ongoing practice.

## Section 4 · Part II. Austin Male Panelist Candidates

Before presenting candidates, I want to report a finding that shaped this section. Austin's tech ecosystem has well-documented women-in-tech organizations — Austin Women in Technology, Latinas in Tech Austin, Girlstart, and Latinitas among them — but the public record of *male* executives actively supporting those organizations is thin. This is not necessarily evidence that such men do not exist; more likely, it reflects that quiet support does not generate press coverage, that the public-facing voices of these organizations are appropriately women, and that our ecosystem has not yet developed the habit of naming and celebrating male allies the way Louisville has.

I identify below the candidates I was able to document from public sources, grouped in tiers by the strength of that documentation. For our purposes, Tier 1 candidates can likely be approached directly based on their public record; Tier 2 candidates warrant outreach but require some verification; Tier 3 represents gaps I recommend our committee close through direct organizational outreach.

### 4.1 Tier 1: Strongly Documented Candidates

#### `TIER 1` — Michael Dell

*Founder, Chairman & CEO, Dell Technologies (Round Rock)*

**Documented Involvement.** In July 2020, Dell publicly called out the technology industry's failure to make adequate progress on diversity and set concrete 2030 targets: 50% of Dell's global workforce and 40% of its global managers to be women. Dell Technologies operates STEMAspire (a 12-month mentoring program for women STEM students) and Dell Career Restart (a return-to-work program for women in technology), and the company has been a consistent partner of Austin Women in Technology. The Michael & Susan Dell Foundation has committed more than $2 billion to education and opportunity programs.

**Why I Would Pursue Him.** As founder of Austin's largest technology employer, Dell's visible participation would signal to the ecosystem that this conversation is not peripheral. His public targets give him concrete numbers to speak to rather than generalities. The practical obstacle is scheduling; I would approach this as a long-shot outreach with a strong fallback.

#### `TIER 1` — Chris Hyams

*Former CEO, Indeed; Board Member, DivInc*

**Documented Involvement.** Hyams serves on the board of DivInc, Austin's accelerator for women and people of color entrepreneurs, and has served as honorary chair of DivInc's Champions of Change Awards, pledging a $30,000 matching donation in one year. He also sits on the board of Huston-Tillotson University, Austin's HBCU. Under his tenure as CEO, Indeed built a dedicated DEIB+ department and was recognized repeatedly by Comparably for diversity practices. He has spoken publicly and unusually candidly about his own learning journey, crediting his wife's Undoing White Supremacy Austin training in 2014 as the beginning of his own education.

**Why I Would Pursue Him.** Hyams is the rare male CEO who discusses his allyship journey in the language of *becoming* rather than *being*. That framing is precisely what I want our audience to hear. He is also actively transitioning his focus toward responsible AI and human rights, which suggests he may have more bandwidth than a sitting CEO.

#### `TIER 1` — Preston L. James II

*Co-Founder & CEO, DivInc*

**Documented Involvement.** James founded the first early-stage tech accelerator in Texas focused exclusively on women and people-of-color founders. DivInc's Startup Sistas initiative serves approximately 300 women-of-color entrepreneurs per year; the organization has helped launch 55+ companies and has since expanded nationally through the Social Justice Innovation Accelerator. James spent 20 years at Dell before founding DivInc.

**Why I Would Pursue Him.** James built an institution to close a documented gap — women and founders of color receive a small fraction of venture capital despite equal business outcomes — rather than speaking about the gap theoretically. His hybrid background (20 years at Dell, then entrepreneurship) lets him speak credibly to both our corporate and startup audience members. As a Black male ally, he also brings an intersectional perspective our previous panels have not always foregrounded.

#### `TIER 1` — Will Griffin

*Chief Ethics Officer, Hypergiant Industries*

**Documented Involvement.** Griffin developed Hypergiant's company-wide AI ethics and diversity framework and has been a consistent public advocate for diverse hiring and funding in technology. His career spans Goldman Sachs, McKinsey & Co., and media entrepreneurship, and he holds a JD from Harvard Law School. He has won an NAACP Image Award for his work.

**Why I Would Pursue Him.** Griffin's positioning at the intersection of AI, ethics, and diversity is unusually timely. As our industry grapples with the consequences of building AI systems on non-diverse teams, his voice will date less quickly than more conventional diversity commentary. His framing captures a business case I want our audience to internalize: diverse hiring is not a peripheral ethical question but a core engineering and product question.

### 4.2 Tier 2: Documented Candidates Warranting Outreach

#### `TIER 2` — Brian Reaves

*Chief Diversity and Inclusion Officer, Dell Technologies*

Leads Dell's MARC (Many Advocating Real Change) program focused on unconscious bias training, oversees the Women in Action ERG and STEMAspire, and manages Dell's diversity recruiting partnerships. As a mid-level-to-senior operational leader rather than a CEO, Reaves may be a more accessible Dell representative than Michael Dell himself, and he brings specific program data rather than aspirational targets.

#### `TIER 2` — Balazs Barna

*Head of US Engineering & Austin Site Lead, Wise*

Leads Wise's 200-person Austin office and has publicly emphasized building diverse teams across all functions. Serves on the Austin Technology Council board. Represents the mid-level engineering leadership demographic that is, in practice, where most of the hiring and sponsorship decisions our audience cares about actually get made.

#### `TIER 2` — Thom Singer

*CEO, Austin Technology Council*

Leads Austin's largest technology industry organization and is a partner to Austin Women in Technology. A practiced public speaker with experience hosting the Austin Tech Connect podcast. Singer brings a useful ecosystem-level view rather than a single-company perspective.

### 4.3 Tier 3: Gaps to Close Through Direct Outreach

My search of public sources did not surface named male executive allies at several Austin-area employers where I would expect them to exist — including Tesla, AMD, SHI International, Microsoft, Amazon, and Google. I recommend our committee close these gaps through direct outreach to the HR or diversity function at each company, asking specifically for the name of a male executive who sponsors the Women in Technology ERG or has been internally recognized for allyship. Similarly, I recommend we contact Girlstart, Austin Women in Technology, Latinas in Tech Austin, and Latinitas to request names of male board members, corporate sponsors, and regular male participants in their programming.

On the question of WGU alumni serving in Austin tech leadership: I found limited public documentation, and I think we should route this through WGU's alumni relations team directly rather than relying on secondary sources.

### 4.4 Recommended Panel Composition

From the candidates documented above, I propose the following four-person composition for our April 2026 panel:

1. **One C-suite executive** from a major Austin employer, to provide institutional legitimacy and permission for mid-level managers in the audience to engage publicly. My first approach would be Chris Hyams; my fallback, Brian Reaves or Michael Dell.
2. **One founder or nonprofit leader**, to bring grassroots credibility and specific outcomes data. Preston James is my first choice.
3. **One mid-level technical leader**, to represent the demographic most of our audience actually reports to. Balazs Barna is my first choice.
4. **One cross-cutting voice** speaking to an emerging dimension of the work. Will Griffin on AI and ethics would give us a panel that dates less quickly than purely inward-facing HR framings.

## Section 5 · Part III. Cross-Case Success Factors

Across the programs reviewed in Part I, eight patterns recur with enough consistency that I would treat them as design principles rather than options. I present them here in rough order of the magnitude of effect I saw in the data.

> **Finding 1 · Highest-Leverage Intervention**
>
> **Personal invitation drives male attendance at gender-focused events.**
>
> In Wallaert's survey of 400 men, 40% of male attendees cited an explicit personal invitation from a colleague as the key factor in their decision to attend. An additional 16% of non-attendees specifically cited not feeling invited as their reason for staying away. The distinction here is between a broadcast email (low effect) and a direct message from a specific colleague that names a specific reason (high effect). If we do nothing else differently for April, this is the intervention I would prioritize.

> **Finding 2**
>
> **Male panelists signal that the event seeks men's participation, not their attendance as spectators.**
>
> Roughly 20% of male attendees in the same survey cited the presence of at least one male speaker as an important factor in their decision. The mechanism is not flattery. It is a practical signal that the event expects men to contribute rather than observe. Our experimental all-male panel directly addresses this signal.

> **Finding 3**
>
> **Explicit welcoming language reduces a documented psychological barrier.**
>
> Catalyst's research on "inhibiting pressures" identifies specific barriers men report facing when considering participation in diversity events: fear of appearing performative, uncertainty about whether their attendance is wanted, and worry about saying the wrong thing. Events that used explicit language — "male allies welcome," "open to all genders committed to this work" — measurably outperformed those that did not. Our registration page and email communications should adopt this language consistently.

> **Finding 4**
>
> **Business-case framing recruits senior leadership; moral framing sustains them.**
>
> Every successful program I reviewed opened with a business-case argument — retention costs, innovation outcomes, competitive positioning — and moved toward moral framing only after credibility had been established. This is not cynicism; it is sequencing. For our audience, the retention data is particularly relevant: replacing a mid-level engineer in the Austin market typically costs 1.5 to 2 times annual salary, and women engineers leave the industry at roughly twice the rate of men.

> **Finding 5**
>
> **Action-oriented content outperforms awareness-building content.**
>
> Participants in MARC's scenario-based workshops — where men practice specific interventions in plausible workplace situations — reported substantially higher satisfaction and post-event behavior change than participants in lecture-format diversity trainings. The practical implication for our April meeting is that the majority of our two hours should be spent on *what to do*, not *why it matters*.

> **Finding 6**
>
> **Research-backed content creates shareable credibility.**
>
> The IWL Summit's partnership with Harvard Business Review gave attendees something to cite in their own organizations. Content that can be referenced by name ("a 2022 HBR study found...") travels further than content that cannot. I recommend we select two or three research findings and repeat them consistently across our pre-event materials, panel discussion, and post-event follow-up.

> **Finding 7**
>
> **Senior leadership visibility provides organizational permission.**
>
> Mid-level managers at Grace Hopper, IWL, and the Women in Manufacturing conference consistently reported that the visible participation of C-suite leaders gave them permission to engage publicly. The implication for our recruitment: even if a senior executive cannot commit to the full two hours, a recorded five-minute opening or a prominent name on our invitation materials produces outsized effect.

> **Finding 8**
>
> **Integration outperforms isolation.**
>
> The male allyship sessions that worked best were embedded in larger women-focused conferences rather than held as standalone men-only events. Our format — an all-male panel within our ongoing quarterly Impact Network cadence — is already aligned with this principle, and I think we should resist any pressure to spin off a separate men's track.

## Section 6 · Part IV. Recommendation for Our April 2026 Meeting

Drawing on the national case studies and cross-case findings above, I propose the following program for our planning committee's consideration.

### 6.1 Positioning and Title

I propose we title the meeting *Allyship in Action: Austin's Male Tech Leaders on the Work of Supporting Women*, with a subtitle or framing line that foregrounds the word *action*. The goal is to set expectations from the invitation forward: this is not a diversity awareness event; it is a working session for men who want practical tools and women who want candid accounts of what their colleagues are actually doing.

### 6.2 Format: Lunch or Evening?

Our committee has discussed moving to an evening format. After reviewing the evidence, I recommend we *retain our lunch format* for this specific meeting, for three reasons:

- **During-work-hours attendance** frames the event as professional development rather than personal commitment, reducing a documented barrier for the male managers we most want in the room.
- **Parents of young children** (men and women alike) face a harder logistical hurdle in the evening than at midday.
- **Our quarterly cadence** has built brand recognition as a lunch event; changing the format and the topic simultaneously introduces two variables at once, which complicates our ability to learn from the result.

I would revisit the evening question for a subsequent quarter, once we have baseline attendance data from April.

### 6.3 Proposed Agenda

| Time | Segment | Purpose |
|:-----|:--------|:--------|
| **12:00–12:15** | Welcome, context, and business case | Set norms, present two or three research findings, frame the two hours as practical rather than theoretical. |
| **12:15–12:40** | Panel introductions and ally journeys | Each panelist takes five minutes to describe the moment they recognized the work, a mistake they made, and one concrete action that produced measurable result. |
| **12:40–1:30**  | Scenario-based discussion | Moderator leads panel through five or six workplace scenarios — meeting dynamics, hiring decisions, sponsorship, compensation equity — with live audience polling and small-group reflection. |
| **1:30–1:50**   | Action planning | Each attendee identifies one or two specific commitments for the next 30 days, using a one-page framework we provide. Optional pairing with a neighbor for accountability. |
| **1:50–2:00**   | Close, resources, and follow-up | Summarize themes, distribute reading list and local organization directory, announce 30-day check-in. |

### 6.4 Sample Scenarios for the Panel Discussion

Based on the MARC framework and the scenarios that generated the strongest engagement in other programs, I would propose we prepare the following for our moderator, selecting four or five based on panelist strengths:

1. **Idea attribution.** A woman proposes an idea in a meeting and receives lukewarm response. Five minutes later, a man makes essentially the same proposal and receives enthusiastic support. What do you do in the moment? After the meeting?
2. **Pattern-matching in hiring.** Your team is evaluating two finalists: one whose background matches the pattern of your previous hires, one whose path is non-traditional but whose potential is strong. Your team is leaning toward the "safer" choice. How do you intervene?
3. **Sponsorship versus mentorship.** You are invited to recommend someone from your team for a high-visibility project. A capable woman on your team would benefit from the exposure but has not asked. What is your responsibility?
4. **Informal dynamics.** At a team happy hour, a colleague makes a comment about a female teammate's appearance that you find inappropriate. How do you respond in the moment? Later?
5. **Compensation equity.** You learn that a woman on your team with comparable experience and performance is paid less than her male peers. What do you do?

### 6.5 Recruitment Strategy: Systematic Invitation Campaign

Rather than relying on our existing distribution list, I propose we run a deliberate personal-invitation campaign in the eight weeks before the event. The mechanics:

- **Six to eight weeks out.** Each current member of our Impact Network personally invites two to three specific male colleagues. We provide a suggested template, but each invitation must include a named reason for the invitation ("I thought of you because...").
- **Four weeks out.** We send personal emails from recognized Austin tech leaders to a curated list of male executives and managers. We publish confirmed panelist names and brief video clips.
- **Two weeks out.** Follow-up invitations. We distribute a short preparatory reading (one article, five minutes) and solicit anonymous questions to inform the panel.
- **One week out.** Final reminder, this time with the business-case framing and any social proof we have accumulated (registered attendee count, notable registrants who have agreed to be named).

If 50 women in our existing network each personally invite two to three men, that is 100 to 150 personal invitations. Historical response rates for invitations of this type range from 25% to 40%; the lower bound would add 25 men to our attendance, the upper bound 60. Either outcome substantially closes the gap to our 10:5 target.

### 6.6 Measurement Framework

I propose we measure the experiment against three distinct outcomes:

1. **Attendance.** Total attendees, male attendees, company representation, and seniority distribution. Baseline: approximately 10% male attendance at recent meetings. Target: 30–35% male attendance.
2. **Engagement.** Post-event survey administered within 48 hours, measuring perceived value, willingness to attend a future meeting, and specificity of committed actions.
3. **Behavior change.** 30-day follow-up survey to male attendees asking which of their committed actions they actually took, what impact they observed, and what obstacles they encountered. Qualitative stories collected and anonymized for future programming.

These metrics should be published internally to our planning committee and shared, in aggregated form, with our audience at a subsequent quarterly meeting. Public accountability for our own experiment is consistent with the accountability we are asking our panelists and audience to model.

## Section 7. Conclusion

The evidence base on male allyship in technology is more mature than I expected when I began this inquiry, and the consistency of the findings across organizations gives me confidence that the design decisions we face are well-characterized. The gap between male intent (97% want to close gender gaps) and male action (roughly 10% attending events like ours) is not a gap of values. It is a gap of invitation, framing, and practice.

Our April 2026 meeting cannot close that gap on its own. What it can do is begin a sustained practice in the Austin ecosystem of inviting men into this work by name, offering them concrete tools rather than abstract commitments, and holding ourselves accountable for measuring the results. If the experiment succeeds on the numbers alone, we will have a useful proof point. If it also seeds an ongoing community of Austin male allies who show up in July and October as well, we will have built something more valuable than a single meeting.

I welcome feedback from our planning committee, our sponsor, and our university partner on any element of the framework presented here. This brief is a working document, and I expect to revise it as our committee deliberates and as panel confirmations arrive.
